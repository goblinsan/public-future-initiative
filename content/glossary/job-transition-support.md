definition: Programs and policies that help workers move from declining industries or displaced roles into new employment, including retraining programs, job placement services, career counseling, relocation assistance, and income support during the transition period. Effective job transition support is typically employer-connected, sector-specific, and funded adequately to cover training costs and lost wages during retraining.

Job transition support covers a spectrum of interventions:

**Retraining programs**: Skills training for workers in declining occupations, ideally connected to employers actively hiring in target occupations. Evidence shows that shorter, credential-based, employer-partnered programs have better outcomes than generic retraining.

**Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA)**: The U.S. federal program providing extended unemployment benefits and retraining funding to workers displaced by trade. Studies of TAA effectiveness are mixed — it provides meaningful support but has not consistently moved workers into comparable employment.

**Sectoral training**: Workforce development organized around specific industry sectors (healthcare, clean energy, advanced manufacturing) with employer networks, defined credentials, and structured career pathways. Considered the highest-evidence model for adult workforce programs.

**Income support during transition**: Extended unemployment insurance or guaranteed income during the retraining and job search period. Without income support, workers cannot afford to participate in training programs that require time away from work.

**Relocation assistance**: Support for workers who would find better opportunities in a different geographic location — addressing the spatial mismatch between where displaced workers live and where new jobs are concentrated.
