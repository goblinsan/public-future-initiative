Guaranteed income proposals vary significantly in design:

**Universal Basic Income (UBI)**: Paid to every citizen or resident, regardless of employment or means. No conditions attached. Often discussed as replacing or supplementing existing welfare programs.

**Negative Income Tax (NIT)**: Proposed by Milton Friedman, this approach guarantees a minimum income through the tax system — those below a threshold receive a payment from the government rather than paying taxes. As income rises above the threshold, the transfer phases out.

**Guaranteed Minimum Income (GMI)**: Targeted at low-income households, ensuring no household falls below a set floor. May include means-testing or work requirements, distinguishing it from UBI.

**Targeted Guaranteed Income Pilots**: Programs like Stockton, CA's SEED initiative provide unconditional cash to low-income residents for a defined period, allowing researchers to study effects on employment, health, and well-being.

Evidence from pilots and existing programs generally shows that guaranteed income recipients do not significantly reduce their work effort and often show improvements in employment outcomes, mental health, and family stability.
