The care economy spans formal and informal sectors:

**Formal care work**: Paid employment in childcare centers, nursing homes, home health agencies, hospitals, and similar organizations. Characterized by low wages relative to skill and educational requirements, high emotional demands, and high turnover.

**Informal and unpaid care**: Caregiving by family members — particularly women — for children, elderly relatives, and people with disabilities. This work is not counted in GDP despite its substantial economic value.

**Why the care economy matters in the transition:**

1. **Automation resistance**: Care work requires physical presence, human empathy, complex judgment in variable environments — features that make full automation technically difficult and socially unacceptable in the near term.

2. **Unmet need**: Demand for care services exceeds supply across most developed economies, creating a genuine labor shortage even as other sectors face automation-driven displacement.

3. **Wage gap problem**: Redirecting displaced workers toward care economy jobs requires confronting the wage gap. Care work pays poorly not because it is low-value, but because of historical devaluation of feminized labor and the difficulty of productivity measurement.

Proposals to strengthen the care economy include public investment in childcare and eldercare infrastructure, wage floors and career ladders for care workers, and recognition of unpaid care in social insurance systems.
