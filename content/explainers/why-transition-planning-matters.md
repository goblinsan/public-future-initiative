## What Is Transition Planning?

Transition planning is the set of deliberate policy choices made in advance of — or in response to — large-scale economic disruption, with the goal of distributing the costs and benefits of change more equitably.

It encompasses:

- **Income support** for people displaced by economic change
- **Skills investment** to help workers move into new roles
- **Community investment** in places disproportionately affected
- **Institutional design** — updating social insurance, benefits, and labor protections to match the new structure of work
- **Ownership and redistribution mechanisms** to share productivity gains broadly

Transition planning is not anti-technology or anti-growth. It is the infrastructure that allows societies to adapt to change without leaving large segments of the population behind.

## What History Teaches

### The Industrial Revolution's unplanned transition

The shift from agricultural to industrial economies in the 18th and 19th centuries was one of the most consequential transitions in human history. Karl Polanyi documented the social destruction of the unmanaged period: traditional communities dissolved, workers urbanized into brutal conditions, and the social fabric of pre-industrial life was torn apart without adequate replacement.

The eventual response — labor protections, public health investment, welfare states, social insurance — emerged through decades of conflict, movement-building, and political struggle. The costs of delay were immense.

### The trade dislocation evidence

Economists David Autor, David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson provided influential evidence that trade-driven labor displacement from Chinese manufacturing competition produced lasting harm in affected U.S. communities — not temporary adjustment as trade theory predicted. Communities that lost manufacturing jobs saw persistent unemployment, lower wages, worse health outcomes, and higher rates of disability claims, poverty, and mortality.

This research matters because it challenges the assumption that workers smoothly re-enter labor markets. When large-scale displacement is rapid and geographically concentrated, the adjustment is not smooth — it is prolonged and devastating without active support.

### The pattern across transitions

The consistent historical pattern is:
1. Economic disruption creates displacement
2. Absent policy response, costs concentrate among the most vulnerable
3. Political instability follows
4. Eventually, new institutions emerge — but only after significant harm

The AI transition is unfolding faster than previous waves. The window for proactive planning is now.

## What Happens Without Transition Planning

The evidence from prior transitions without adequate policy response is consistent:

### Income falls and stays down
Workers displaced from good manufacturing or clerical jobs do not simply find equivalent work. They typically experience permanent wage reductions, longer periods of unemployment, and retirement with lower savings and weaker Social Security claims.

### Health outcomes worsen
Job loss is associated with increased rates of depression, anxiety, substance use disorders, and premature mortality. The "deaths of despair" literature documents elevated mortality among working-age Americans in communities that experienced industrial decline — a public health crisis with direct economic roots.

### Communities hollow out
When anchor employers close, the effects cascade. Local businesses that served those workers close. Population outmigration follows. Tax bases erode, reducing public service quality. Civic institutions weaken. Recovery without deliberate investment is rare.

### Political destabilization
Economic displacement without adequate response fuels political alienation, grievance, and the appeal of authoritarian and nationalist politics. This pattern — documented across multiple countries following trade-driven and automation-driven job loss — represents a systemic risk beyond economic metrics.

## Why the AI Moment Is Different

The current wave of AI-driven disruption differs from prior transitions in ways that make proactive planning more urgent:

### Speed
Industrial transitions unfolded over decades. Some AI-driven changes in clerical, administrative, and analytical work are occurring over years. The time available for individual adjustment and policy development is compressed.

### Breadth
AI affects cognitive work — which was previously a safe harbor from automation. No educational credentials or professional status guarantees insulation from AI capability in adjacent tasks.

### Concentration of gains
Unlike earlier periods where productivity growth translated into broad wage growth, the current economy is structured to concentrate productivity gains among capital owners and a small tier of high-skill workers. The automatic mechanisms that once spread growth more broadly are weakened or absent.

### Global asymmetry
AI capabilities are concentrated in a small number of firms in a small number of countries. The regulatory, tax, and redistribution capacity of national governments to respond is asymmetric with the global scale of the actors involved.

## What Proactive Planning Looks Like

Effective transition planning operates on multiple timescales:

### Immediate: Income floors and stabilizers
Strengthen unemployment insurance. Explore portable benefits that follow workers rather than attaching to jobs. Consider pilot programs for income floor policies like guaranteed income or UBI.

### Near-term: Retraining investment
Build workforce development programs that are employer-connected, sector-specific, and funded at scale. Close the gap between available job training and what displaced workers actually need.

### Medium-term: Social insurance modernization
Update social insurance systems designed for the industrial economy to cover gig workers, platform workers, and people outside formal employment. Delink health insurance, retirement savings, and other benefits from employment status.

### Long-term: Ownership and distribution
Build mechanisms for broad participation in capital returns. Public wealth funds, worker ownership programs, and automation dividend revenue sharing address the structural gap between capital and labor income.

## The Stakes

The choice is not between disruption and stability — automation is happening regardless of policy. The choice is between managed and unmanaged transition: who bears the costs, whether communities survive the adjustment period, and whether the productivity gains of AI are shared widely enough to justify the disruption they cause.

Planning is not guaranteed to succeed. But the absence of planning is guaranteed to concentrate harm.
