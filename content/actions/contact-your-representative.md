## Why This Works

MPs, TDs, and councillors pay attention to constituent contact — especially when it's specific, personal, and persistent. A well-written letter that identifies a real local impact and asks a concrete question is far more effective than a mass-email campaign or a petition with ten thousand signatures.

Research from the Hansard Society and equivalent Irish bodies consistently finds that **personalised, evidenced contact from individual constituents** is the most effective form of citizen pressure short of voting.

## Step 1: Find Your Representative

**UK — Members of Parliament:**
Visit [TheyWorkForYou](https://www.theyworkforyou.com/) or the [House of Commons search](https://members.parliament.uk/) and enter your postcode.

**Republic of Ireland — TDs:**
Use [Oireachtas.ie](https://www.oireachtas.ie/en/members/) and filter by constituency.

**Local councils (UK):**
Visit [WhoDoIVoteFor.co.uk](https://whocanivotefor.co.uk/) and enter your postcode.

**Local councils (Ireland):**
Your county council website lists all elected councillors by electoral area.

## Step 2: Write an Effective Letter

A good letter to a representative:
1. **Opens with your local connection** — "I am a constituent in [ward/constituency]…"
2. **Names one specific issue** — not five, not vague concerns, one
3. **Cites evidence briefly** — "According to [source], [fact]…"
4. **Asks a specific question** — "Will you support [bill/motion/inquiry]?"
5. **Closes with your contact details** — MPs are required to respond to constituents

Keep it under one page. Bullet points are fine. Formal but not stuffy.

## Template Letter

> Dear [Name],
>
> I am writing to you as a constituent in [constituency]. I am concerned about [specific policy issue] and would like to ask for your position on [specific proposal or question].
>
> [One or two sentences of relevant evidence — cite the source.]
>
> [One sentence connecting the issue to local impact — e.g., "In our area, [X number] of households are affected by…"]
>
> I would be grateful if you could [specific ask — e.g., "support the Second Reading of the Guaranteed Income Pilot Bill" or "raise this issue at the next council planning committee"].
>
> I am happy to provide further information or meet to discuss this.
>
> Yours sincerely,
> [Your name]
> [Your address]

## What Happens Next

Most MPs and TDs aim to respond within two to four weeks. If you don't receive a response, a polite follow-up is appropriate.

If your representative gives a vague or dismissive response, you can:
- Ask them to clarify their position
- Raise their response in a letter to a local newspaper
- Share their response (with permission or as a public record) on social media
