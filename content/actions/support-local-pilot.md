## What "Supporting a Pilot" Actually Means

A local pilot is not a protest or a campaign. It's a structured test of an idea — designed to generate honest evidence, not to demonstrate that a policy works.

Supporting a pilot responsibly means:
- Helping it happen with integrity, not just with enthusiasm
- Not overpromising outcomes to participants
- Accepting that the evidence may not support replication at scale
- Making sure evaluation is built in from the start, not added as an afterthought

## Types of Local Initiatives Worth Replicating

**Guaranteed income and income support pilots:**
Several UK councils and Irish local authorities are exploring this. The Stockton SEED model and the Welsh Basic Income Pilot for care leavers are the most directly replicable examples.

**Community land trusts:**
A model for permanently affordable housing that removes land from the speculative market. Already operating in London, Bristol, Leeds, and other cities.

**Local food and resource sharing networks:**
Community fridge networks, repair cafes, and tool libraries have a strong evidence base for resource reduction and community cohesion. Relatively low-cost to start.

**Participatory budgeting:**
Inviting community members to allocate a portion of a council's discretionary budget. Used successfully in Porto Alegre, Paris, and several UK councils.

## How to Connect With Existing Networks

Don't start from scratch. Connect with:
- [Participatory City](https://www.participatorycity.org/) — practical participation infrastructure
- [Community Land Trust Network](https://www.communitylandtrusts.org.uk/) — replication support for CLTs
- [Feeding Britain](https://feedingbritain.org/) — food security and community fridge networks
- [New Economics Foundation](https://neweconomics.org/) — research and support for local economic pilots

## What to Do Before You Start

Before making any public commitment:
1. **Talk to the council.** Even if you don't need their permission, you'll need their cooperation.
2. **Map the existing landscape.** Is someone else already doing this? Can you support them rather than duplicate?
3. **Build a steering group.** Include people who are sceptical of the idea as well as enthusiasts.
4. **Define your evaluation from day one.** What will you measure? Who will do the measuring independently?
5. **Be honest with participants.** Tell them this is a test, not a guaranteed long-term programme.
