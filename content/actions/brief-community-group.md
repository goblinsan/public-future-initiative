## Why Briefing Your Community Group Matters

Organised community groups — trade unions, faith congregations, parent associations, residents' groups, charity trustees — are the connective tissue of civic life. When these groups understand a policy issue in evidence-based terms, they become a much more powerful force for change than individual citizens acting alone.

A 20-minute briefing at your next meeting can shift the conversation from vague unease to concrete, informed advocacy.

## Before You Present

**Know your audience.** A trade union branch has different concerns from a residents' association or a church congregation. Tailor your framing — the same facts land differently when connected to what your audience already cares about.

**Choose one topic.** Don't try to cover everything. Pick the policy area most relevant to your group and stick to it.

**Use the materials we provide.** Our briefing packs are designed to be honest about uncertainty, not to persuade people to a particular view. That's what makes them credible with sceptical audiences.

## What a Good Briefing Includes

A 20–30 minute slot should cover:
1. **What's happening (5 minutes)** — the key facts about the issue, sourced and honest
2. **What options exist (10 minutes)** — the main policy approaches, with their evidence and trade-offs
3. **What others have tried (5 minutes)** — one or two real-world examples (pilots, policies in other countries)
4. **What you can do (5 minutes)** — one concrete next step your group can take together

## Handling Difficult Questions

You will be asked questions you can't answer. The right response is: "I don't know — let me find out and come back to you." This is more credible than a confident wrong answer.

You may also face ideological pushback — someone who sees the issue as a left/right battleground. The best response is to return to the evidence: "That's a legitimate view — here's what the evidence says about that approach."

You are not there to win an argument. You are there to make sure your group has good information.

## After the Briefing

A good brief ends with a decision, not just a discussion. Invite your group to take one collective next step:
- Write a joint letter to your MP or TD
- Invite a local councillor to your next meeting
- Host a fuller community discussion using our host-your-own guide
- Sign up to be kept informed as the issue develops
