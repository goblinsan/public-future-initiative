## The US Healthcare Anomaly

The United States spends approximately twice as much per capita on healthcare as comparably wealthy countries — yet ranks near the bottom on population health metrics including life expectancy, infant mortality, and management of chronic conditions. This anomaly sits at the centre of the single-payer debate: if other systems spend less and achieve more, what do they do differently, and could the US replicate it?

## What Single-Payer Systems Have in Common

Single-payer healthcare — in its purest form — replaces multiple private and public insurers with a single government payer that covers all or most healthcare costs for all residents. The defining features are:
- **Universal coverage**: Everyone is enrolled; no uninsured population.
- **Unified administration**: One set of billing standards, forms, and reimbursement rules.
- **Monopsonistic purchasing power**: The single payer negotiates prices with providers and drug manufacturers from a position of strength.

Existing single-payer or near-single-payer systems include Canada, Taiwan, South Korea, and (for most services) the UK's NHS.

## The Administrative Savings Argument

The most robust empirical finding in comparative health systems research is the extraordinary administrative cost of the US multi-payer system. A frequently cited study (Woolhandler et al., 2003) estimated that US administrative costs were roughly double those of Canada — and more recent estimates suggest this gap has widened. Billing complexity, prior authorisation, claims adjudication, and insurance marketing consume resources that, in single-payer systems, go to direct care.

The single-payer case rests heavily on the claim that these administrative costs could be largely eliminated, generating savings sufficient to fund universal coverage.

## The Implementation Complexity Argument

The counterargument does not typically deny that other countries achieve good outcomes at lower cost. Instead, it questions whether those outcomes are attributable to payer structure specifically, and whether the US could replicate them.

Several complications arise:
- **Social determinants**: US outcomes on life expectancy are significantly affected by gun violence, obesity rates, and socioeconomic inequality — factors outside any health system's direct control.
- **Transition costs**: Moving 160 million people from employer-sponsored insurance to a single-payer system would require restructuring provider reimbursement, managing employment transitions in the insurance sector, and navigating powerful political opposition.
- **Innovation trade-offs**: The US pharmaceutical and medical device industry benefits significantly from US market pricing; reduced pricing power under single-payer could affect R&D investment globally, not only domestically.

## What the Evidence Shows

The evidence supports several conclusions with moderate confidence:

- **Administrative savings are real and large**: Multi-payer administrative overhead is a genuine cost driver in the US system, and single-payer systems do achieve substantially lower administrative costs.
- **Universal coverage improves population health outcomes**: The evidence that insured populations have better preventive care uptake, earlier diagnosis, and better chronic disease management is robust.
- **The US comparison is confounded**: Comparing US outcomes to those of other countries requires controlling for social determinants that differ across countries for non-healthcare reasons.
- **System design details matter**: Single-payer is not a monolithic policy — outcomes depend on coverage scope, payment rates, managed care arrangements, and public health investment.

## Open Questions

- Can the administrative savings from single-payer be realised without equivalent cost-shifting to other parts of the healthcare system?
- How should the transition from employer-sponsored insurance be managed to avoid disruption to existing coverage?
- What drug pricing and innovation policy should accompany a single-payer reform to balance access and R&D incentives?
- How much of the US-international outcomes gap is attributable to health system design versus social determinants outside the health system?
