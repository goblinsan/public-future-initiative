## The 2050 Constraint

The scientific consensus — expressed through the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report — requires global net-zero CO₂ emissions by approximately 2050 to limit warming to 1.5°C. This is not a gradual transition: it requires near-complete decarbonisation of energy, transport, buildings, and industry within roughly 25 years. The question is which policy instruments make that achievable.

## The Case for Carbon Pricing

Carbon pricing — whether as a tax or a cap-and-trade system — makes greenhouse gas emissions costly, directly incentivising firms and households to reduce them. The theoretical argument is compelling: it reaches every economic actor simultaneously, rewards whoever can reduce emissions most cheaply, and generates revenue.

Evidence from implemented schemes supports moderate confidence in this mechanism. British Columbia's carbon tax, introduced in 2008, reduced emissions relative to counterfactual while maintaining GDP growth. The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), after a period of near-zero prices due to oversupply, has become more effective as the cap has tightened.

## The Case for Regulation and Investment

Critics note that carbon pricing has rarely been implemented at levels sufficient to drive the required speed of transition — and that politically sustainable prices have been far below what models suggest is necessary. The gilets jaunes protests in France and the repeal of Australia's carbon price illustrate the political fragility of carbon pricing mechanisms.

More fundamentally, some of the most important decarbonisation decisions — the structure of electricity grids, urban planning, building retrofit programmes — are not primarily price-sensitive. They require coordinated public investment, regulatory standards, and industrial policy.

The US Inflation Reduction Act, passed in 2022, is widely cited as evidence that direct public investment and tax incentives for clean energy can drive faster deployment than carbon pricing in the US political context. Within two years of passage, clean energy investment had increased dramatically, driven by production tax credits rather than a carbon price.

## What the Evidence Shows

The most credible assessments conclude that no single instrument is sufficient:

- Carbon pricing is most effective at driving marginal emissions reductions and fuel-switching in power generation.
- Regulatory standards are more effective for setting floors (vehicle efficiency, building codes) and phase-out schedules for specific technologies.
- Direct public investment is essential for early-stage technologies, grid infrastructure, and decarbonisation pathways where private investment underserves.
- International carbon border adjustments (like the EU's CBAM) may be necessary to prevent carbon leakage as domestic carbon prices rise.

The debate is not simply "carbon price vs. regulation" but what combination, sequenced how, with what distributional safeguards.

## Open Questions

- At what carbon price level does behaviour change substantially, and is that level politically achievable?
- Can carbon dividend schemes adequately offset the regressive impacts of carbon pricing?
- What is the right balance between price signals and direct investment in accelerating the energy transition?
- How should developed countries support decarbonisation in emerging economies where the policy toolkit differs?
