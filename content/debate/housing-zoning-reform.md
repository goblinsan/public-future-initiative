## The Housing Crisis in Context

Housing affordability has deteriorated sharply across most high-income countries since the 1980s. In many major cities, housing costs absorb more than 30–40% of household income for renters on median wages — and far more for low-income households. The question is not whether a crisis exists, but what caused it and which interventions can fix it.

## What Zoning Has to Do with It

Zoning — the legal rules governing what can be built where — shapes the supply of housing in a city more than almost any other factor. In most major Anglophone cities, large areas of desirable, well-serviced land are zoned exclusively for low-density single-family homes. This restricts the supply of housing in the places people most want to live.

The restrictive zoning explanation for unaffordability has attracted support from economists across the political spectrum: building restrictions in productive cities impose enormous costs on household welfare, restrict labour mobility, and concentrate the gains of urban productivity among homeowners.

## The Counter-Argument: Supply Does Not Reach Those Who Need It Most

Critics of supply-focused reforms make a different diagnosis. The most severely cost-burdened households — those in the bottom income quintile — cannot afford market-rate housing at any realistic supply level. The housing crisis they experience is a crisis of poverty as much as a crisis of supply, and no amount of market-rate construction will produce homes affordable at minimum wage.

On this view, supply reform is a solution to the problems of middle-income households, not the deeply poor. The policy required is direct public investment in below-market housing — social housing, community land trusts, or housing vouchers — not zoning liberalisation.

## What the Evidence Shows

The evidence is genuinely mixed:

- **On supply and prices**: Multiple natural experiments (Minneapolis' 2040 plan, Auckland's upzoning, Tokyo's minimal zoning restrictions) show that increased supply moderates rent growth compared to counterfactual restricted-supply cities. However, effect sizes are contested and timelines are long.
- **On filtering**: The "filtering" mechanism — whereby new units eventually become affordable as they age — is empirically documented but slow. Studies suggest filtering timelines of 20–40 years, offering no relief to current renters in crisis.
- **On displacement**: The relationship between new supply and displacement is contested. Some studies find that new construction reduces displacement pressure; others find that it concentrates investment in ways that accelerate gentrification in specific neighbourhoods.
- **On social housing**: Countries with strong social housing sectors (Austria, Netherlands, Denmark) consistently achieve better housing outcomes at lower cost-burden ratios. However, these systems were built over decades and face different political economies than Anglophone countries.

## The Open Questions

- Does the filtering mechanism work fast enough to help households in current crisis?
- Can zoning reform be designed to prevent displacement alongside supply expansion?
- What combination of supply-side and demand-side interventions produces the best outcomes?
- How should social housing be financed in fiscal environments hostile to public spending?
