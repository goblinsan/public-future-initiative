## What Is the Shorter Work Week Proposal?

The shorter work week proposal would reduce the legal or normative standard for full-time work from 40 hours per week to 32 (or 30) hours, with no proportional reduction in pay. Workers would receive the same weekly wages for fewer hours. This is distinct from part-time work (fewer hours, proportionally less pay).

## The Historical Argument

The 40-hour work week was not inevitable. It emerged from decades of labor organizing and was codified in U.S. law in 1938 (Fair Labor Standards Act). Before that, 60–80 hour work weeks were common. The reduction to 40 hours corresponded with major productivity growth from industrialization — productivity gains were shared partly as higher wages and partly as shorter hours.

Automation represents a similar productivity inflection point. The question is whether gains are shared as leisure, wages, or ownership — or captured entirely as profit.

## What the Evidence Shows

**UK 4-Day Week Pilot (2022)**
- 61 companies, 2,900 workers, 6-month trial
- 92% of companies continued the policy post-trial
- Revenue unchanged (up 1.4% on average)
- Sick days declined 65%; staff turnover dropped 57%
- 71% of employees reported reduced burnout

**Iceland 4-Day Week Trials (2015–2019)**
- 2,500 public sector workers across 66 workplaces
- Productivity maintained or improved
- Led to renegotiated union contracts covering 86% of Iceland's workforce

**Limitations of Current Evidence**
- Participating companies are self-selected (more likely to succeed)
- Most trials are in knowledge work / office environments
- Short-term trials may not capture long-term equilibrium effects
- No randomized controlled trials at national scale

## Who Benefits and Who Doesn't

The shorter work week primarily helps currently employed, full-time workers. It does not directly address:
- Workers already displaced by automation
- Part-time or precarious workers who need more hours, not fewer
- Workers in the informal economy

Its role in automation response is distributional — it converts productivity gains into leisure time rather than unemployment — but it is not a substitute for income support policies.

## Implementation Challenges

- **Sectoral heterogeneity**: Healthcare, hospitality, and emergency services face structural constraints on hour reduction.
- **Global competition**: Sectors facing international competition may face real cost pressures from higher per-unit labor cost.
- **Transition support**: Small employers may need subsidy or technical support to redesign workflows.
- **Overtime rules**: U.S. overtime law (44+ hours) would need reform to align incentives.

## Open Questions

- At what pace of phase-in is disruption manageable?
- How should sectoral carve-outs be designed to avoid exempting most of the economy?
- How does shorter work week interact with rising use of AI-assisted knowledge work that blurs work/non-work boundaries?
- What happens to workers who want to work more than the new standard?
