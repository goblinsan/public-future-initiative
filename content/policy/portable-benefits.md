## What Are Portable Benefits?

Portable benefits are worker protections — health insurance, retirement savings, paid leave, training funds — that are attached to the individual worker rather than to a specific employer. They accumulate across multiple jobs, contract periods, and forms of employment, and do not disappear when a worker changes jobs or takes on freelance work.

## The Problem With the Current System

The U.S. benefit system was designed for the mid-twentieth century employment relationship: a single employer, long tenure, full-time work. That model describes a shrinking share of workers:

- **Job tenure** has declined. Median tenure is 4.1 years across all workers and 2.8 years for workers aged 25–34.
- **Gig and contract work** has grown. An estimated 36% of U.S. workers participate in the gig economy in some form.
- **Automation-driven displacement** accelerates transition between jobs and industries.

Workers who fall outside stable employment relationships lose health coverage, stop accumulating retirement savings, and have no access to paid leave or retraining funds.

## How Portable Benefits Work

The core mechanism is a **benefit account or pool** to which employers, platforms, and workers contribute in proportion to work performed:

- A gig worker completing rides for Uber and deliveries for DoorDash accumulates benefit credits from each platform.
- A full-time employee moving between jobs carries benefit credits from previous employers.
- A freelancer contributing quarterly estimated taxes also makes benefit contributions to their personal account.

Pooling mechanisms — industry-wide funds or regional pools — achieve group pricing and risk-sharing that individual accounts cannot.

## Precedents and Experiments

- **Denmark's flexicurity**: High-quality publicly funded unemployment benefits and active labor market policies combined with flexible hiring/firing rules. Workers experience transitions without catastrophic benefit loss.
- **Freelancers Union proposals**: Advocacy organization Freelancers Union has proposed portable benefit pools for over a decade, with partial implementation in some states.
- **Senate Bills**: Several U.S. Senate bills have proposed portable benefit accounts, none of which have passed as of 2024.

## The Gig Economy Debate

Portable benefits intersect with the contested question of worker classification. Gig companies classify workers as independent contractors, avoiding benefit obligations. Some portable benefits proposals would require contributions without changing classification — which critics argue legitimizes contingent work rather than addressing it. Others see portable benefits as a pragmatic floor while classification battles continue.

## Open Questions

- Should portable benefits replace employer-sponsored benefits or supplement them?
- What contribution rate ensures benefit adequacy rather than just portability?
- How should administration be structured — individual accounts, industry pools, or public funds?
- Does enabling portable benefits accelerate the growth of contingent work?
