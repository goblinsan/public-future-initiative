## The Proposal: Public Ownership of Wealth-Producing Assets

Prime Minister Mark Carney announced his government is considering **a sovereign wealth fund that would own and grow public assets for the benefit of all Canadians**. The fund would operate as a national investment vehicle whose profits could help pay for a basic income.

Carney framed it as a way to **capitalize public assets** and then use the returns to support **guaranteed income**. In essence, the proposal socializes capital gains: the public owns the assets and the profits help fund cash payments to citizens.

## Key Distinction: Ownership vs. Income

This is a crucial policy distinction:

- **Ownership-based redistribution** (sovereign wealth fund): The public owns productive assets and receives a share of returns
- **Income-based redistribution** (universal basic income): The government redistributes cash directly to citizens

A public wealth fund addresses a different problem than UBI, though both can contribute to income security:

| Dimension | Sovereign Wealth Fund | Universal Basic Income |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Mechanism** | Owns and invests in productive assets | Direct cash transfer |
| **Who benefits** | All citizens as asset owners | All citizens equally |
| **Funding source** | Returns on capital | Tax revenue or program consolidation |
| **Timeline** | Long capitalization period | Immediate payment |
| **Scalability** | Limited by asset base and returns | Limited by government budget |

## Why This Matters in a Post-Work Society

Automation and digital technology are concentrating wealth among a small number of capital owners. Workers whose labor trained AI systems and contributed to productivity growth receive no ownership stake. A sovereign wealth fund offers a structural response: give citizens a stake in the productive assets that generate returns.

This is why Canada's proposal matters beyond a single policy:

- **Automation may increase productive output**, but currently, profits flow to shareholders
- **Capital concentration may widen inequality** without intervention
- **Labor displacement may raise social strain** without broad income support
- **Shared prosperity may require decommodifying basics** (housing, healthcare, education)

A public wealth fund is one mechanism for broadening access to returns from productive assets, complementing other policies like portable benefits and public services.

## Concrete Examples: How This Works

### Alaska Permanent Fund
Alaska's Permanent Fund has distributed annual dividends ($1,000–$2,000) to all residents since 1982, capitalized by oil revenues. Every year, each resident receives a check. This demonstrates the viability of citizen dividend models.

### Norway's Government Pension Fund Global
Norway's $1.4 trillion "Oil Fund" invests North Sea oil revenues globally and funds Norwegian public services. While it doesn't pay direct dividends to citizens, it demonstrates how sovereign wealth funds can generate durable income streams.

### Canada's Opportunity
Canada would follow a similar model — but capitalized differently, and potentially distributed as citizen dividends rather than through public spending. The key question is: **What assets would the fund hold, and how would it be capitalized?**

## The Bigger Picture: Multiple Tools for Multiple Problems

A public wealth fund is not a silver bullet. It works alongside other policies:

- **Sovereign wealth fund** = public ownership of capital
- **Universal basic income** = unconditional cash support
- **Guaranteed income** = guaranteed floor payment
- **Portable benefits** = reduce costs of essentials
- **Public services** = broaden access to necessities

The strongest approach likely combines multiple tools. A public wealth fund addresses ownership and concentration; UBI or guaranteed income addresses immediate income needs; public services address access to healthcare, education, and housing.

## Next Steps for Canada

The proposal is still under consideration, but it reflects a growing recognition that:

1. **Automation creates winners and losers** — policy should share gains more broadly
2. **Ownership structure matters** — public ownership of productive assets can generate public revenue
3. **Durable solutions require structural change** — not just transfers, but changes to the underlying distribution of wealth
4. **Multiple mechanisms work together** — a mix of public wealth funds, income support, and public services is stronger than any single tool
