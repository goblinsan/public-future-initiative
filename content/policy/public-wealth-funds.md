  - question: How would an automation fund be capitalized?
    answer: Proposed mechanisms include a tax on robot deployments (e.g., per-unit automation tax), a levy on digital advertising or data usage, mandatory equity stakes in large AI companies at IPO or above a market cap threshold, and reallocation of existing corporate tax revenues to a citizen fund rather than general government spending.
  - question: Who manages the fund and how is governance ensured?
    answer: Governance models range from independent central bank-style boards with legislative oversight to direct citizen governance through cooperative structures. The key risks are political capture (governments raiding the fund), short-termism, and conflicts of interest when the fund holds stakes in regulated industries. Norway's model uses strict governance rules and prohibition on domestic investment as a conflict-of-interest barrier.
  - question: Would this replace other income support programs?
    answer: Proponents generally position public wealth fund dividends as a supplement, not a replacement, for existing income support. The dividend is a return on public ownership, not a welfare payment. This framing has helped build cross-partisan support in some contexts.

## What Are Public Wealth Funds?

Public wealth funds (also called citizen wealth funds or sovereign wealth funds) are publicly managed investment vehicles that hold diversified assets on behalf of citizens and distribute returns as dividends or fund public services. Unlike traditional government spending, they accumulate capital that grows over time.

## The Core Problem They Address

The productivity gains from automation, AI, and digital technology accrue primarily to shareholders and senior executives rather than to workers or the broader public. This is not a natural law — it reflects ownership structure. If the public held equity stakes in the companies that benefit from automation, public wealth would grow alongside private wealth.

## How They Work

1. **Capitalization** — The fund acquires assets through taxes, mandatory equity stakes, or initial public offerings of publicly owned enterprises.
2. **Investment** — Assets are invested in diversified portfolios of equities, bonds, and real assets, managed for long-term return.
3. **Distribution** — Returns are distributed as annual or quarterly dividends to all eligible citizens, or used to fund public services.

## Precedents

- **Alaska Permanent Fund** (1982–present): Every Alaska resident receives an annual dividend from a fund capitalized by oil revenues. Annual payments have ranged from $331 to $2,072.
- **Norway's Government Pension Fund Global**: Holds $1.4 trillion in global assets, funded by North Sea oil revenues. Returns fund Norway's public services rather than paying direct dividends.
- **Singapore's Temasek Holdings**: State-owned investment company managing $380+ billion in assets, though not a direct-dividend model.

## Automation Dividend Variant

A specific application of this model would create a fund capitalized by taxes on automated production — sometimes called an "automation tax" or "robot tax." The rationale is that automation replaces taxable labor (payroll taxes, income taxes), and a direct levy on automation deployment could replace some of this lost revenue while creating citizen ownership of productivity gains.

## Implementation Challenges

Building a fund large enough to pay meaningful dividends takes decades. In the near term, the fund may be more valuable as a governance and ownership instrument than as an income source. Political sustainability over decades requires careful design of governance rules that insulate the fund from budget pressures.

## Open Questions

- Should capitalization be compulsory (mandatory equity stakes) or voluntary (tax-funded acquisition)?
- How should fund governance be structured to prevent political capture?
- At what fund size do dividend payments become meaningful as income support?
- How should the fund interact with existing public pension systems?
