  - question: Is UBI the same as a negative income tax?
    answer: Not exactly. A Negative Income Tax (NIT), advocated by Milton Friedman, provides a guaranteed minimum income only to those below a threshold, phasing out as income rises. In practice, a sufficiently high UBI combined with taxation produces similar effective distributions, but they differ in administrative design and political optics.

## What Is Universal Basic Income?

Universal Basic Income (UBI) is a policy in which every adult citizen receives a regular, unconditional cash payment from the government. It is universal — paid to everyone regardless of employment, income, or behavior — and unconditional — no work requirements, no asset tests, no reporting obligations.

## The Core Design Choices

The word "UBI" covers a wide range of proposals. The key variables are:

- **Payment level** — $500/month covers partial needs; $1,500–$2,000/month approaches a livable floor. Higher payments cost more and require more aggressive funding.
- **Universality** — Truly universal programs pay everyone including the wealthy (who pay it back through taxes). Means-tested versions reduce cost but reintroduce poverty traps.
- **Funding** — Wealth taxes, financial transaction taxes, carbon dividends, or sovereign wealth fund returns each carry different distributional effects and political barriers.
- **Program interaction** — Whether UBI replaces or supplements existing programs dramatically changes its impact on the most vulnerable.

## What Pilots Have Shown

The strongest current evidence comes from randomized trials in Stockton (California), Finland, and several programs in East Africa and Canada:

- **Employment**: Participants maintain or increase employment; the "work disincentive" feared by critics is not strongly supported in current evidence.
- **Mental health**: Consistent improvements in wellbeing, reduced anxiety, and better health outcomes.
- **Spending**: Cash is used primarily for food, housing, utilities, and education — not alcohol or luxury goods.
- **Limitations**: Most pilots are small-scale, time-limited, and not representative of macro conditions under a universal permanent program.

## Distributional Impact

Who benefits most depends on how UBI is funded and whether it replaces existing programs. A well-designed UBI funded by progressive taxation would primarily redistribute income downward. A poorly designed UBI that eliminates targeted benefits for disabled people or single parents could leave them worse off.

## Political and Implementation Landscape

UBI has supporters across the political spectrum — libertarians see it as a simple replacement for complex welfare bureaucracy; progressives see it as a floor against poverty; tech-sector voices frame it as a response to automation. This broad appeal has not translated into legislative momentum in the U.S., where it faces opposition from both conservatives (cost, work disincentives) and left advocates (risks to existing programs).

## Open Questions

- At what payment level does UBI become an adequate floor rather than a supplement?
- Can macro effects (inflation, labor market restructuring) be adequately modeled before national rollout?
- How should transition from existing programs be managed to protect the most vulnerable?
- What political coalition can sustain the fiscal commitment over decades?
