## Overview

Preston, a post-industrial city in Lancashire, England, was written off as a failing economy in the early 2010s. A major retail development had collapsed; unemployment was high; investment had dried up. Instead of waiting for outside investment, Preston City Council, working with the Centre for Local Economic Strategies (CLES), developed a different approach: redirect the spending power of local anchor institutions — the city's hospitals, university, college, and council — towards local and regional suppliers.

The Preston Model, as it became known, is now cited internationally as a leading example of community wealth building.

## What Was Tested

The experiment asked: can local public institutions meaningfully redirect their procurement to local suppliers, and does that improve local economic outcomes?

Starting around 2012, Preston Council analysed where anchor institutions in the city were spending money and found that most of it left the local economy. Working with each institution, they identified categories of spending that could feasibly be redirected to local and regional businesses, worker cooperatives, and public enterprises.

## What Happened

Between 2012 and 2017, the proportion of Preston's anchor institution spending retained in the city rose from 5% to 18%. Lancashire-wide retention rose from 39% to 79%. In concrete terms, this meant hundreds of millions of pounds being spent with local suppliers rather than national or international chains.

Preston climbed from one of the worst-performing cities in the UK on economic measures to one of the most improved. By 2018 it was ranked the most improved city in the UK for quality of life by PricewaterhouseCoopers and Demos.

New worker-owned cooperatives were established with support from the council, and a community bank was created to provide local investment.

## Key Findings

- Anchor institution spending retained **locally rose from 5% to 18%** between 2012 and 2017
- Preston went from being one of the **most deprived** UK cities to ranking among the **most improved** by 2018
- The strategy involved **no new public money** — only redirecting existing institutional spending
- Multiple **worker cooperatives** were established with council support
- The model has been studied and partially replicated in cities including **Cleveland (USA), Everett (USA), and Amsterdam (Netherlands)**
