## Overview

From January 2017 to December 2018, the Finnish government ran the largest government-administered basic income randomised controlled trial in Europe. 2,000 unemployed Finns aged 25–58 were randomly selected to receive €560 per month with no conditions attached — regardless of whether they found work. A control group of 173,000 similarly unemployed people received standard unemployment benefits.

The experiment was designed and administered by Kela, Finland's Social Insurance Institution, in partnership with the University of Helsinki.

## What Was Tested

The experiment asked: would a modest unconditional payment improve the well-being and employment prospects of unemployed people, compared with the existing conditional benefit system?

Recipients could take any job, start a business, or do nothing — the €560 continued regardless. Bureaucratic reporting requirements were eliminated for participants.

## What Happened

Employment among recipients increased slightly compared with the control group — 6 more days of employment per year on average. The effect was modest but statistically significant.

The more striking findings were in well-being. Recipients reported significantly lower levels of stress, depression, and anxiety. They reported greater confidence in their ability to influence their own lives. Trust in other people and in public institutions was measurably higher among recipients.

Participants described relief from the psychological burden of conditional benefit systems — the constant reporting, the fear of sanctions, the stigma of being means-tested.

## Key Findings

- Recipients worked **6 more days per year** on average than control group members
- Significant improvements in **mental health, well-being, and trust in institutions**
- Recipients reported **less bureaucratic stress** and greater sense of autonomy
- The experiment demonstrated that basic income does **not increase passivity**
- Confidence, social trust, and life satisfaction all improved among recipients
